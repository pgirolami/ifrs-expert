# Goal

This is a manual experiment made during the implementation of [document routing](../../plans/2026-04-08--document-selection-stage-plan.md). We ingested all the IFRS free corpus and ran  against the Q1 family of questions to check which documents are returned most often.

A [script](./run_q1_document_routing.py) was used to automate capturing quantitative data across sub experiments. It runs
```bash
for document_type in IFRS IAS IFRIC SIC PS; do
  printf '<question>' | uv run python -m src.cli query-documents --document-type "${document_type}" --min-score 0.3 -d 30 --json
done
```
across all questions in [family Q1](../00_QUESTIONS/Q1/family.yaml), merges the per-type results, and produces the tables.

We expect IFRS 9 and IFRIC 16 to score high.

Every sub experiment changes the embeded document representation so we had to re-ingest the corpus. We optimized the `store` command to be able to only store the documents table & FAISS index.

### Analysis

- IFRS 9 is consistently visible and highly-ranked
    - it was retrieved for every question
    - its median rank is 1 and average 1.6
    - its minimum score is 0.53 but its average is 0.55

- IFRIC 16 is not consistently visible and its ranking is way too low
    - it wasn't retrieved in the top 30 for Q1.1 and Q1.2
    - its average rank & median rank was 16th
    - its minimum score went down to 0.49

That being said, IFRS 9 does explicitely mention a "net investment hedge" in section 6.5.2 and refers to IAS 21.
>There are three types of hedging relationships:
>
>   (a) fair value hedge: a hedge of the exposure to changes in fair value of a recognised asset or liability or an unrecognised firm commitment, or a component of any such item, that is attributable to a particular risk and could affect profit or loss.
>
>   (b) cash flow hedge: a hedge of the exposure to variability in cash flows that is attributable to a particular risk associated with all, or a component of, a recognised asset or liability (such as all or some future interest payments on variable-rate debt) or a highly probable forecast transaction, and could affect profit or loss.
>
>   (c) hedge of a net investment in a foreign operation as defined in
IAS 21.

However, section 5 of "IAS 21 The Effects of Changes in Foreign Exchange Rates"  explicitely says IAS 21 does not apply to hedges
> This Standard does not apply to hedge accounting for foreign currency items, including the hedging of a net investment in a foreign operation. IFRS 9 applies to hedge accounting.

Earlier, IFRIC 16 was necessary to consistently identify the approaches but maybe with the better retrieval of IFRS 9 chunks that the current approach is promising, it won't be.

### Conclusion

- The likely issue for the discrepancy between IFRS 9 and IFRIC 16 is not only lexical mismatch on specific terms such as “dividend”, but more generally that the current document representation favors direct topical matches over indirect but doctrinally important alternatives.

- The next step should focus on improving the visibility of alternative-path documents such as IFRIC 16, either by enriching their routing representation further or by introducing a retrieval strategy that better preserves alternative routes alongside the main mechanism document.
